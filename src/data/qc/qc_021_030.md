## scenario_021: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **Numbers accuracy — internal math inconsistency in step 2:** Step 2 claims "if 20% of 75M standard subscribers downgrade from $15.49 to $9.99+$7 = $16.99, revenue per downgrader actually increases." This math is applied incorrectly in the step — the downgrader was previously paying $15.49, and now pays $9.99, a $5.50 loss per subscriber in subscription revenue. The step correctly notes ad revenue of $7 makes the net a gain of $1.50, but the sampleResponse (Step 2 script) says "Netflix loses about $5.50 per subscriber in subscription revenue per month. That's $82.5 million per month" — this implies 15M downgraders × $5.50 = $82.5M, which is correct. However, the script then says "net loss per downgrader is only $5.50 minus $7" — but the $15.49 was the price paid, not $14 (the average used elsewhere). The sampleResponse mixes the $15.49 list price and the $14 average ARPU inconsistently without acknowledging this.
2. **Numbers accuracy — ARPU comparison is ambiguous:** The context states "75M US subscribers at an average of $14 per month." The standard plan is listed as $15.49. The $14 average implies a mix of plan tiers. Step 1 of stepByStep and the sampleResponse both compare the new ad-tier ARPU of $16.99 against the "$14 standard average" — but the breakeven should arguably be compared to the $15.49 list price (since the 75M are on the standard plan). Using $14 vs. $15.49 affects the conclusion. The scenario never clarifies this.
3. **MECE integrity — acceptable but incomplete:** The three meceBlocks (Revenue Per Ad-Supported Subscriber, Cannibalization & Downgrade Risk, Competitive Positioning) are reasonably MECE. However, there is no block for **Content/Licensing Constraints** — the scenario explicitly raises the risk that some titles can't appear on the ad tier, but this is buried inside Cannibalization rather than treated as its own structural issue affecting subscriber value.
4. **keyTerms — "ACV (Ad-Supported Content Value)" is a fabricated term:** ACV in media/entertainment contexts is not a standard industry term meaning "Ad-Supported Content Value." This definition appears to be invented. In media, ACV typically means "Annual Contract Value." The scenario uses this term to refer to content library availability on the ad tier, which is not a recognized industry term. This could actively mislead students.
5. **Sample response realism — Step 2 script contains a minor confusing sentence:** "net loss per downgrader is only $5.50 minus $7, which is actually a gain of $1.50 per user" is correct but reads awkwardly — subtracting a larger number from a smaller one to show a net positive is confusing. A real interviewer would simply say "the $7 in ad revenue more than offsets the $5.50 subscription loss."
6. **Framework fit — "Profitability Tree" is listed as a correctFramework but not used with any visible tree structure:** The stepByStep uses math calculations but never draws a profitability tree structure or walks through revenue branches explicitly. The description of "Profitability Tree models the revenue equation" is effectively just arithmetic, not a structured tree decomposition.

**Worst offender:** The "ACV (Ad-Supported Content Value)" keyTerm is a fabricated industry term that doesn't exist and will mislead students into using it in real interviews where they will be corrected.

This is technically the revenue *at current price* for those 80K units — it doesn't represent lost revenue from the price increase correctly. Lost revenue from volume decline = 80K × $5.95 (new price), not $5.45. The calculation conflates "revenue lost from losing volume" with "revenue at old price for that volume." The actual daily revenue loss from 80K fewer units at $5.95 = $476K, not $436K.
2. **Numbers accuracy — breakeven threshold calculation in stepByStep Step 1 is wrong:** "At 8M lattes/day × $5.45 = $43.6M daily revenue. A $0.50 increase with zero volume loss = $47.6M." This is incorrect. 8M × $5.95 = $47.6M. That's right. But then: "The breakeven volume decline: revenue must stay at $43.6M, so $43.6M ÷ $5.95 = 7.33M units — a 8.4% decline." Wait — 1 - (7.33/8) = 8.4%. But (8 - 7.33)/8 = 8.375%, so rounding to 8.4% is fine. The math here is actually correct. However, the sampleResponse script says "That's $43.6 million divided by $5.95 — which gives me about 7.33 million units, or a decline of 8.4%." The calculation 43.6 / 5.95 = 7.328M, so "7.33M" and "8.4% decline" are both correct. Step 1 is numerically sound. **Retract: this is not an error.**
3. **Numbers accuracy — Step 2 Rewards member math check:** "55% of 8M = 4.4M Rewards transactions. At 3% decline: lose 132K units. 45% = 3.6M non-members. At 9% decline: lose 324K units. Total volume loss = 456K units, or 5.7%." Verification: 4.4M × 3% = 132K ✓. 3.6M × 9% = 324K ✓. 456K / 8M = 5.7% ✓. Math is correct.
4. **keyTerms — "ACV (All Commodity Volume)" definition misapplied and flagged:** The keyTerm for "ACV (All Commodity Volume)" reads "Not applicable here — in this context, 'captive market' refers to locations like airports..." This is bizarre. If ACV is not applicable, it should not be in the keyTerms section at all. Including a term with "Not applicable here" as its definition is unhelpful and confusing. However, there is no block for **Brand/Cumulative Perception Risk as a standalone structural concern** — cumulative 28% price increase is mentioned in keyConsiderations but the meceBlocks fold it into the competitive dynamics block implicitly. It deserves its own block given it's identified as the key long-term risk.
6. **Clarifying questions — one is generic:** "Has consumer research been done on the cumulative price perception — is there a threshold above which customers actively switch rather than grumble?" — This is a legitimate question but is somewhat leading and generic. A more specific version would ask what the NPS trend line has been over the past four years of price increases.

The "Pricing Framework" in correctFrameworks is somewhat decorative here.

**Worst offender:** Including "ACV (All Commodity Volume)" as a keyTerm with the definition "Not applicable here" is a clear error — it should be removed from this scenario's keyTerms entirely.
