## scenario_065: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **The $750M operating profit decline is internally inconsistent with the margin percentages:** The margin declined from 18.2% to 14.6% — a 3.6 percentage point decline. On $21B current revenue: 3.6% × $21B = $756M ≈ $750M. However, this calculation assumes revenue was already $21B when margins were at 18.2% — but revenue grew 9% year-over-year. If $21B is the current year and prior year was ~$19.27B, the actual profit decline is 18.2% × $19.27B minus 14.6% × $21B = $3.51B - $3.07B = $440M. The $750M figure is the "margin at current revenue scale" framing, not the actual year-over-year profit change. This is not clearly explained to students and could be confused for a simple year-over-year loss.
2. **Digital revenue "$10B" is assumed:** Step 2 says "assuming digital DTC is roughly half of the $21 billion — call it $10 billion." The prompt does not give the digital/physical split. This is flagged in the clarifying questions, which is correct — but then the sampleResponse proceeds to do math on $10B anyway. Students need to be aware this is an assumption, not given data.
3. **The "Merchant Discount / MDR" key term definition is wrong for this context:** In a DTC profitability scenario, the MDR definition provided ("the contribution margin after product cost and fulfillment, before fixed overhead allocation") is not the standard definition of MDR. MDR is the merchant discount rate charged by card networks — not a DTC contribution margin metric. This conflates two different terms and will confuse students. The term listed should be "Contribution Margin" or "Gross Margin per order," not MDR.
4. **Return rate math is correct:** 83M orders, 10pp increase = 8.3M extra returns, $25-30 per return = $200-250M ✓
5. **Framework fit is correct:** Profitability Tree is the right and only framework. Used well.
6. **MECE blocks are clean:** Revenue Mix / Fulfillment & Returns / Physical Retail — mutually exclusive, no gap.
7. **Clarifying questions are excellent:** The footwear-specific return rate question is sophisticated and case-relevant.
8. **Interviewer expectations are correct:** Separating mix effect from cost drivers before recommending solutions is the key discipline tested.

**Worst offender:** The "MDR" key term definition is factually wrong for this scenario context — it defines a card network concept using a DTC contribution margin definition, which is an error that will actively mislead students.

## scenario_066: FLAG (Critical)
**Issues:**
1. **CRITICAL — Mathematical contradiction in the prompt itself:** The prompt states (a) lounge operating costs increased 67% over 3 years, (b) cardholder usage grew 120%, and (c) per-visit cost is now $42 vs. $28 three years ago (a 50% increase). These three numbers are mathematically incompatible. If visits grew 120% (new visits = 2.2× original) and per-visit cost grew 50% (new cost = 1.5× original), then total costs grew 2.2 × 1.5 = 3.3× original — a 230% increase, not 67%. No combination of the three figures (67% cost increase, 120% visit increase, 50% per-visit cost increase) is internally consistent. The correct relationship given a 67% cost increase and 120% visit increase would be a per-visit cost DECREASE of 24% (from $28 to $21.27), not an increase to $42.
2. **Step 1 attempts to paper over the contradiction but fails:** Step 1 says "with visits up 120% but costs up only 67%, variable cost per visit is actually more efficient at scale" — this correctly identifies that costs/visit should have fallen, but then the prompt says costs/visit ROSE to $42. Step 1 does not resolve this; it just notes the discrepancy and moves on.
3. **Fabricated numbers for retention NPV calculation:** Step 2 uses "5 million heavy lounge users" and "8 average annual visits per user" — neither number appears in the prompt or context. The $1.68B operating cost figure derived from these ($42 × 8 visits × 5M users) is entirely constructed.
4. **Amex Centurion lounge count inconsistency:** The prompt says 40 locations. The conclusion mentions expanding to "60 lounge locations." The logic is reasonable but the target number is unsourced.
5. **Framework fit is correct:** Profitability Tree + 4P's — the 4P's application is somewhat forced (the scenario is fundamentally about access policy and capacity, not classic 4P marketing mix) but defensible.
6. **MECE blocks are solid:** Cost Driver / Revenue & Value Offset / Capacity & Access Policy — clean, no overlap, collectively exhaustive.
7. **Clarifying questions are excellent:** The "fixed vs. variable cost split" and "was guest access expanded?" questions are sharp and case-specific.
8. **Interviewer expectations correctly identify the key trap:** Anchoring in the $1,200 retention value before proposing any restrictions is genuinely what distinguishes strong candidates.
9. **The Gold cardholder fee recommendation:** The sample recommends "$50 per-visit fee" — the context says Gold cardholders "currently allowed 2 free visits per year" which is different from what the step describes as "2 free annual visits then $50 fee." The wording is consistent, but the context says Amex "could add a per-visit fee for Gold cardholders (currently allowed 2 free visits per year)" — this reads as Gold currently gets 2 free visits, and Amex could add fees on top of that. The recommendation interprets this correctly.

**Worst offender:** The three core numbers in the prompt (67% cost increase, 120% visit increase, $28→$42 per-visit cost increase) are mathematically incompatible. This is a foundational data integrity failure that will confuse any quantitatively sharp candidate who tries to reconcile the numbers.

The statement "that 3-point blended gross margin decline from mix alone on $1.4B = $42M... explains roughly half of the 6-point operating margin decline" mixes gross margin and operating margin without acknowledging the distinction.
