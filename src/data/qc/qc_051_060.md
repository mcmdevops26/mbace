Not wrong, but slightly disjointed.

Appears to be a copy-paste artifact from a different scenario. The question as asked ("Is Nike's margin per unit healthy enough to fund marketing investment") would be relevant but the Tide reference is an error.

## scenario_052: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **CRITICAL MATH ERROR in step 1 and sampleResponse.** The calculation reads: "5% of inactive members to one stay per year at an average of $150/night for 2 nights = $321M." Actual math: 5% × 107M inactive members = 5.35M members. 5.35M × $150 × 2 nights = $1.605 billion — not $321M. The $321M figure is off by a factor of ~5. The error appears to come from an intermediate miscalculation — possibly computing $150 × 2 = $300 but then applying some wrong denominator. The sampleResponse repeats this as "about $320 million," perpetuating the error. A student presenting "$320M" to an interviewer while showing the stated inputs would fail a basic math check instantly.
2. **"Reducing churn" framing is imprecise.** This is a loyalty program scenario, not a subscription churn scenario — the scenario measures "active rate" (38%), not monthly churn. Yet the framework choice calls it "3C's Growth Framework" and the steps address "churn" without establishing a clear definition. Loyalty programs don't use churn terminology the same way subscription services do. This muddies the analysis.
3. **Missing MECE block: increasing revenue per active member.** The three blocks are (1) Reactivate inactive 62%, (2) Increase nights per stay for active members, (3) Drive direct booking via app adoption. Blocks 2 and 3 are not mutually exclusive — "increase nights per stay" and "drive direct booking via app" heavily overlap. A member who books via app also tends to stay longer. These could be collapsed or restructured. The missing block is something like "Expand non-hotel revenue / partnership ecosystem" which is a major lever discussed in the sample response but has no dedicated MECE block.
4. **Conclusion math is unanchored.** "If we move the active rate from 38% toward Marriott's 45% even halfway, we're talking roughly $300 to $400 million in incremental annual revenue." This range is presented without showing the calculation. Given the $321M error in step 1, the reader cannot trust the $300-$400M figure either.
5. **RevPAR in keyTerms is orphaned.** RevPAR is defined in keyTerms but never mentioned in the prompt, context, MECE blocks, stepByStep, or sampleResponse. It does not appear in the scenario body at all. Including it without any use context adds noise without value.

**Worst offender:** The $321M math error is catastrophically wrong (off by 5x). If a student presents this calculation verbatim in an interview, they will fail the quantitative portion immediately. This must be corrected.

## scenario_053: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **Math error in conclusion.** The sampleResponse conclusion states: "If we can recover frequency from 5.7 to 6.0 purchases per year across 56 million buying households at the current price point, that's roughly $90M in recovered revenue annually." Actual math: (6.0 - 5.7) = 0.3 additional purchases × 56M households = 16.8M additional units × $1.80/unit = $30.24M — not $90M. The $90M figure is approximately 3x too high. There is no path to $90M from the stated inputs. This error is in the memorable "closing number" of the sample response — exactly what students are likely to memorize and repeat.
2. **"8% decline" in purchase frequency phrasing is technically correct** ((6.2-5.7)/6.2 = 8.06%) but framing it as "a 8%" is a grammar error. Should be "an 8%."
3. **MECE blocks are solid but "Perception vs. Product Reality Gap" overlaps with "Brand Architecture Decision."** The first block concludes that perception is the problem; the second block debates how to fix the brand. These are sequential, not parallel. A better structure would be (1) Diagnosis: Where is volume leaking?, (2) Consumer Segment Prioritization, (3) Strategic Response Options. The current structure front-loads the conclusion (perception is the problem) before the evidence is fully presented.
4. **Clarifying questions are case-specific and good.** No issues.
5. **Key terms — "Price elasticity" definition is partially circular.** It defines price elasticity as "how much purchase volume changes in response to a price increase" — that's only one direction of elasticity (price up → volume change). Standard definition should include both directions (price up or down).

**Worst offender:** The $90M conclusion math error is 3x the correct figure. This is the student's takeaway number and it's wrong.

The keyConsiderations say "What investment in brand awareness is needed to make CALIA a recognized brand?" — implying the decision is open — but the sample response closes it definitively without showing the debate.
3. **Nike relationship management is the most distinctive element of this case, and it's handled well.** The category conflict framing is realistic and the recommendation to create a "Nike protected zone" is specific and actionable.

## scenario_056: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **Minor math discrepancy in churn recovery estimate.** Step 1 states: "Reducing churn from 4.2% to the Walmart+ rate of 1.8% would recover approximately $111M annually." Actual math: the difference in monthly churned subscribers = 6.3M × (4.2% - 1.8%) = 6.3M × 2.4% = 151,200 subscribers retained per month. But this isn't quite the right comparison — Instacart's $6.99 is the per-delivery fee with Instacart+, not the non-subscription rate. Instacart's non-subscription delivery fee is typically $5.99-$9.99 per order depending on basket size. Comparing Walmart's non-subscriber rate to Instacart's subscriber rate is misleading.
3. **Framework choice is strong.** Profitability Tree → 3C's is well-motivated and applied correctly. Starting with the math before solutions is exactly right.
4. **MECE blocks are clean.** Churn Reduction, New Subscriber Acquisition, and Competitive Positioning are genuinely non-overlapping and exhaustive.
5. **Clarifying question 4 is excellent and case-specific** — distinguishing grocery delivery churners from Walmart+ overall churners is a nuance most candidates would miss.
