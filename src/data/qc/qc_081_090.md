# QC Report: Scenarios 081–090

**Reviewer notes:** All math was verified by hand. Numbers in context are taken as given; issues are flagged where internal numbers contradict each other or where numbers are fabricated without basis in the provided context.

## scenario_081: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **Numbers fabrication — retail revenue equivalence ($34B vs. $36B, both wrong):** The stepByStep (Step 4) states $10B ad revenue is "equivalent to adding $36B in retail revenue in profit terms." The sampleResponse says "$34 billion" for the same math. Actual calculation: $10B × 72% GP = $7.2B GP; $7.2B / 23% retail margin = $31.3B. Neither $34B nor $36B is correct — both figures are fabricated. This is the scenario's featured quantitative insight and it's wrong in both places and inconsistent between the two places it appears.
2. **MECE block subissue error:** The "Strategic Risks" block asks whether Walmart can "attract 900K advertisers from Amazon's 900K" — the goal is to grow Walmart's own advertiser base, not poach Amazon's specific 900K advertisers. This misframes the competitive challenge.
3. **CPG retail media conflation:** The meceBlocks ask "What share of the $8B CPG retail media pool could Walmart win from Amazon and Google/Meta?" — CPG retail media budgets go to retail platforms (Walmart Connect, Amazon Ads), not to Google/Meta. Conflating retail media with general digital advertising is a category error.
4. **Framework fit — SPECIAL-T/MVM vs. 3C's:** The stepByStep does not visibly structure analysis around SPECIAL-T dimensions; it reads as a standard growth strategy assessment. The 3C's is mentioned in Step 2 but not applied as a distinct framework step.
5. **Clarifying question #3 is generic:** "Is Walmart's margin per unit currently healthy enough to fund increased marketing investment, or is P&G expecting this brand to be a cash cow right now?" This references P&G — Walmart Connect is Walmart's own business, not P&G's. Appears copy-pasted or hallucinated from the Tide scenario (scenario_001).

**Worst offender:** The retail revenue equivalence math is wrong in both places it appears ($36B in steps, $34B in sample response) and neither matches the correct answer of ~$31.3B. A student studying this would learn and cite incorrect arithmetic.

## scenario_083: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **Fabricated market growth rate:** Step 3 states "The global biosimilar market is growing at 25%+ annually." This figure does not appear in the context and is presented as fact without qualification. Industry estimates for biosimilar market growth range from 12-25% depending on source and time period — presenting "25%+" as a reliable fact is overconfident.
2. **Enbrel revenue ($4B+) is asserted, not given:** Step 1 says "Enbrel at $4B+" — Enbrel's US revenue is approximately $3-4B depending on year, but this is not stated in the context. A student could not have derived this from the provided information; it's implicit analyst knowledge presented as a given fact. The prompt only says the top 5 biologics generate $11B combined.

It's plausible industry knowledge but stated as a certainty without basis in the provided materials.
4. **Pipeline bridge math never closed:** Step 5 says "if the pipeline can generate $4-6B in new revenue by 2030, combined with $3-4B in biosimilar growth, Amgen replaces most of the $8B cliff" — but $4-6B + $3-4B = $7-10B, which is more than "most" of $8B; at the midpoint it's essentially full replacement. The characterization as "most" undersells what the math actually implies.
5. **MECE gap — manufacturing/supply chain block missing:** Value Chain Analysis is listed as a correctFramework, but none of the meceBlocks is titled around the value chain. The manufacturing cost advantage (a core strategic asset) is buried as a subissue in the "Biosimilar Business Growth" block rather than getting its own MECE block as the framework would demand.

**Worst offender:** The global biosimilar growth rate of "25%+ annually" is stated as fact but is not supported by the context. It's a defensible number but presented with false precision, and a student citing it in an interview would be on shaky ground.

## scenario_085: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **Internal numerical inconsistency — shrink cost doesn't verify from other given numbers:** The context states "the additional 0.6% shrink rate costs approximately $180,000 per store annually." Let's verify: Scan & Go annual revenue per store = 1,178 Scan & Go transactions/day × $94 avg transaction value × 365 days = ~$40.5M. 0.6% of $40.5M = $243,000 — not $180,000. The stated $180K doesn't reconcile with the stated transaction value ($94) and adoption rate (31%). The scenario accepts $180K uncritically and builds the entire breakeven analysis on it, producing a -$34K net that's likely wrong by ~$60K if derived from first principles. The "correct" breakeven adoption rate also changes.
2. **Breakeven math contingent on the wrong shrink figure:** The entire analytical centerpiece — the 38% breakeven adoption rate and the -$34K per-store economics — is derived from the $180K figure that doesn't reconcile with the transaction data. If shrink cost is actually $243K, the net economics are -$97K/year per store (worse than stated) and the breakeven adoption rate rises to ~50%, which dramatically changes the recommendation.
3. **$94 average transaction value — causality not addressed in steps:** The meceBlocks acknowledge "Is the $94 vs. $68 transaction value caused by Scan & Go, or does Scan & Go attract higher-spending customers regardless?" but the stepByStep never addresses this in the recommendation. The steps use transaction value in the shrink calculation but ignore the selection bias question when evaluating the revenue upside.
4. **Amazon Fresh reference is imprecise:** "Amazon Fresh has pulled [Just Walk Out] from several locations due to accuracy issues." This is accurate as of 2024 but the scenario doesn't note that Amazon's reasons for pulling the technology included both accuracy issues and reliance on remote human labelers — the "accuracy issues" framing is incomplete. Minor but the scenario positions this as a clear cautionary parallel that isn't quite analogous (Amazon Fresh uses computer vision + weight sensors; Scan & Go is customer self-scan — different failure modes).

**Worst offender:** The $180K shrink cost is internally inconsistent with the $94 transaction value and 31% adoption rate — and the entire featured breakeven analysis is built on that inconsistent number. The breakeven adoption rate (38%) and per-store economics (-$34K) are both wrong if derived from first principles.

## scenario_086: PASS
**Issues:**
1. **Phrasing confusion in Step 1 — "72% of non-adopters":** Step 1 opens with "The 72% of non-adopters break into: 44% awareness gap, 31% reliability failure..." This is ambiguous. The correct statement is: "Non-adopters represent 72% of eligible guests. Their barriers break down as 44% / 31% / 18% / 7%." The current phrasing could be read as "44% of the 72%" which would make the percentages incorrectly scaled. The sampleResponse handles this correctly, but the stepByStep creates potential confusion.
2. **Duplicate sentence in context field:** The context field repeats "Hilton app has 22 million downloads but only 9.4 million monthly active users" verbatim twice. Minor data quality issue but signals copy-paste error in the source data.
3. **Phase adoption targets in conclusion are optimistic without justification:** The sampleResponse says awareness fixes alone move adoption from 28% to 34% (converting 15% of the 44% awareness bucket). 15% of 44% of guests = 6.6 percentage points. But not all 44% are eligible or reachable by email — the scenario gives no churn or eligibility data to validate the conversion rate estimate. The 15% conversion assumption is stated without basis.
4. **Key terms — NPS definition is slightly off:** The keyTerms NPS definition says it "ranges from -100 to +100" — technically NPS is calculated as a score, not a bounded range, though -100 to +100 are the theoretical extremes. The definition is technically correct but oversimplified.

**Worst offender:** The duplicate sentence in context is the most obvious quality signal, but none of the issues are severe enough to materially mislead a student. This scenario is among the stronger ones in the set.

## scenario_087: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **$65 average transaction value is fabricated:** Step 1 states "If the average transaction value is approximately $65 (global average)" — this number is not in the context and is marked as an assumption. The fraud savings calculation ($4.3B annually) is entirely dependent on this unverified figure. If the actual global average MasterCard transaction is closer to $45-50 (which industry data suggests for card-present retail), the fraud savings drop to $3.0-3.3B. This is a foundational number for the core financial analysis and it's made up.
2. **Payback calculation in Step 2 is inconsistent:** Step 2 concludes "the payback is measured in months if biometric achieves meaningful transaction volume" then says "Against $430M in fraud savings at 10% conversion, the subsidy pays back in roughly 18 months." 18 months = $680M / ($430M/year) = 1.58 years = 19 months, not 18. The math is close but slightly stated inaccurately. More importantly, the 10% conversion assumption is never justified — why 10%?
3. **MECE gap — no block for network effects / ecosystem dynamics:** MasterCard's business is fundamentally a two-sided network (merchants + consumers). The meceBlocks address consumer trust and merchant adoption separately but don't have a block for the network effect interaction: adoption only reaches critical mass when both sides simultaneously cross a threshold. This is the core strategic challenge in payment network expansion and it's not captured as a MECE block.
4. **Completion rate recommendation in Step 3 ($97%+) is fabricated:** "Recommend the engineering team set a minimum 97% completion rate threshold before accelerating." This threshold is presented as a specific standard, but it's invented. No basis in context or industry benchmarks is provided.
5. **Sample response has only 4 sections:** The stepByStep has 5 steps but the sampleResponse has only 4 labeled sections (Opening, Clarifying, Step 1 Fraud Savings, Step 2 Subsidy/Payback, Conclusion). Steps 3-5 covering completion rate, trust gap, and the final recommendation are all compressed into the Conclusion. The analytical work in Steps 3-5 is the differentiating part and it's rushed in the sample.

**Worst offender:** The $65 global average transaction value used to calculate the $4.3B fraud savings headline is fabricated — it's the foundation of the financial case and it's an unverified assumption presented without range or sensitivity analysis.

**Issues:**

But the scenario also states 80 pilot hospitals are already deployed at $180K/year = $14.4M in existing revenue. This is counterintuitive — Cardinal has 80 deployed hospitals, so it's clearly ahead in deployment. The "head start in development" presumably means McKesson started building its platform 12 months ago, but Cardinal is further along. The phrasing implies McKesson is ahead when Cardinal clearly isn't.

The numbers need support.

This question appears to be copied from the Amgen scenario (083) and is entirely inapplicable here. Cardinal distributes medications; it doesn't manufacture them.

**Worst offender:** The final clarifying question about biosimilar manufacturing facilities is clearly copy-pasted from the Amgen scenario and is factually wrong for Cardinal Health (a distributor). This is a significant content error that would confuse students.

**Issues:**
1. **Hinge Health valuation is dramatically wrong:** Step 4 says "Hinge Health at $300M in revenue is potentially acquirable for $1-3B." Hinge Health's last known private valuation was approximately $6.2B (2021 funding round at $6.2B valuation). The scenario's $1-3B range is off by 2-6x and would mislead a student who cites this in a case interview. The scenario uses this fabricated range to argue that "partnership" is cheaper than an acquisition — which may still be true, but the valuation anchor is materially wrong.

But it doesn't actually estimate whether $200M is sufficient for all three of these — it just lists the activities. The scenario never validates whether $200M is a reasonable build budget, which is a critical assumption underlying the entire investment decision.

## scenario_090: PASS
**Issues:**
1. **Missing quantification of default rate impact on the portfolio:** The scenario notes "14% lower default rates among approved applicants" but never calculates what this means for the $8.4B portfolio. A full model would show: if 23% more loans are approved and those marginal borrowers default at (say) 6% vs. the base 3.2%, the net credit loss impact partially offsets the $380M NII gain. Step 2 raises this question ("what is the projected default rate specifically for borrowers approved by AI but rejected by FICO?") but doesn't provide a framework for answering it. The scenario sets up a good analytical question and then doesn't model it.
2. **$380M NII estimate is accepted without stress-testing:** The $380M figure is given as a context fact and is never triangulated. If the current $8.4B portfolio generates roughly $380-450M in NII (at typical personal loan spreads of 4-5%), then a 23% increase in approvals adding $380M in new NII would roughly double the portfolio's NII — implying either a very high interest rate environment or that the marginal borrowers carry much higher rates. This doesn't fail on its face, but the scenario never checks whether $380M is internally consistent with the portfolio size.
3. **Delayed NII framing in Step 4 is slightly misleading:** "The 8-month delay costs approximately $253M in delayed NII ($380M × 8/12)" — this frames the 8-month compliance build as "costing" $253M, but that's only true if the model is ready to deploy today and only the compliance work is holding it back. The scenario doesn't confirm this; the $253M is a theoretical cost of delay, not a verified foregone revenue figure.
4. **No definition of "ECOA" in keyTerms:** The scenario's entire regulatory discussion hinges on ECOA (Equal Credit Opportunity Act), which is mentioned repeatedly in the steps and sampleResponse, but ECOA does not appear in the keyTerms definitions. A student unfamiliar with banking regulation needs this term defined.

**Worst offender:** The absence of ECOA in keyTerms is a meaningful gap given that it's the central regulatory constraint discussed across multiple steps and the entire compliance framework. Everything else in this scenario is clean — the math checks out, the framework is genuinely applied, and the sampleResponse is realistic and specific.
