## scenario_031: FLAG
**Issues:**
1. **Numbers accuracy — fabricated revenue figure.** The `sampleResponse` states "P&G's US business is roughly $50 billion," and then derives "$39 billion coming through...retailers." P&G's total annual global revenue is roughly $80B; the US segment is closer to $18–20B historically. The $50B US figure appears to be conflated with global revenue. This makes the "$780 million in revenue at risk" calculation built on a fabricated number that an interviewer familiar with P&G could immediately challenge. The `prompt` and `context` do not specify P&G's US revenue, so the $50B figure was invented by the response writer — and it's wrong.
2. **MECE integrity — "Strategic Rationale" block partially overlaps Operational block.** The third sub-issue in "Strategic Rationale & Objectives" ("What first-party consumer data does P&G gain...") is essentially a sub-issue of operational feasibility — you don't need a separate block to ask about data value; it could sit under any of the three blocks without being missed. Minor but the blocks are not cleanly exclusive.
3. **Framework fit — four frameworks listed, but only two are meaningfully applied.** `correctFrameworks` lists 3C's, 4P's, Porter's Five Forces, and Market Entry Framework. The `stepByStep` applies 3C's logic (Step 1 implicitly) and Market Entry options (Step 4). Porter's Five Forces and 4P's are invoked in `whyThisFramework` but never actually used in the `stepByStep` or `sampleResponse`. The response does not walk through any of the Five Forces explicitly, nor does it use 4P's structure. Listing four frameworks when two are actually used teaches the student bad habits — they'll try to juggle four frameworks in an interview.
4. **Clarifying questions — mostly solid but Q1 is too granular for an opening.** Asking "Which product categories would be included in the initial DTC bundle" as the first clarifying question is operationally focused when the strategic question (whether to launch at all) hasn't been answered yet. Strong openers in case interviews ask the "why" before the "what."
5. **Sample response realism — the conclusion is strong.** The closing is specific, numbers-backed, and well-reasoned. Opening framing is natural. Overall the script reads like a real MBA candidate.
6. **Interviewer expectations — well-written and correctly identifies the critical constraint.** Appropriately flags retailer retaliation as the thing a weak candidate misses.

**Worst offender:** Fabricated $50B US revenue figure for P&G used to derive all downstream risk calculations — if an interviewer knows P&G's financials, this instantly damages credibility.

The math works but the phrasing "translating to ~6 points of gross margin" glosses over the mechanics (10.8% cost increase on a 52% base = 5.6 gross margin points, close to 6).
